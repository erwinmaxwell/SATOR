Other transport systems

The two systems reviewed previously, namely circulatory and respiratory, are the most classical examples of animal transport systems. However, quite a few other animal structures can be categorised as such.

Lymphatic System

Most animals possess another essential transport system, allowing for circulation of overflown blood plasma, components of the immune system and fat.

As man took a closer look at mammalian circulation, it observed many strange things. Ancient anatomists found about the previously mentioned blood circulatory system in that way, but also about a whole other parallel system, full of a milky fluid which was eventually called lymph.

Timeline of evolution of knowledge on lymphatic system anatomo‐physiology
(Natale et al., 2017)

As opposed to cardiovascular circulation, this lymphatic system is open in all animals, in the sense that the circulation isn't sealed off from the organism everywhere, so the fluid can diffuse in certain areas (much like haemolymph in some invertebrates). In fact, this open characteristic of the lymphatic system is key to its main function, maintaining fluid balance in tissues.

N.B. : the adjective corresponding to lymph is lymphoid, while lymphatic is specific to the circulatory system of lymph fluid.

As blood flows through capillaries, it irrigates tissue as some blood plasma, packed with the nutrients needed by tissue cells, seeps through small pores in the vessels. This liquid finds its way in the tissue itself, where it is known as interstitial (or extracellular) fluid. Some of this extra plasma, once its resources have been depleted, flows back in the blood capillaries, but not at a rate sufficient to stop accumulation. If this reserve of fluid builds up, it leads to a condition known as hypovolemia, a reduction of the volume of circulating blood, which may lead to hypotension, acidification of the blood and eventually heart failure. To avoid this unfortunate condition, the random luck of evolution has gradually elaborated a variety of small lymphatic capillaries, able to drain this interstitial fluid from tissues, and to redirect it to blood circulation by use of a network of vessels, propelled by small muscles. Along the lymphatic vessels, a series of valves avoid reflux, as the smaller vessels lead to greater ones, called lymphatic trunks, eventually opening on to veins.

This lymphatic circulation, essential for fluid balance, also serves other capital purposes.

First, thanks to a variety of characteristic organs, the lymphoid system as a whole plays a very significant role in the organism's immune system. Indeed, the production of all lymphocytes, the major cellular components of the adaptive immune function, representing about a quarter of the circulating white blood cells in human, are generated and maturated in the lymphatic system. Furthermore, the lymph circulation is used by all kinds of other immunitary cells and antibodies as a transport system. Organs implicated in this function of the lymphatic system are classified as primary or secondary.

Human lymphatic system
Cancer Research UK / Wikimedia Commons

Primary (or central) lymphoid organs are the sites of production of lymphocytes, where immature cells are differentiated into specific white blood cells. Organs categorised as such include bone marrow, thymus, fetal liver, and in birds a structure called the bursa of Fabricius. These structures vary in different animals, but their global roles is always the differentiation of cells into immune cells known as lymphocytes, which come in two main flavours, B and T. This process will be elaborated on in the dedicated chapter.

Secondary (or peripheral) lymphoid organs take care of further maturation and maintenance of B and T lymphocytes produced by the primary organs, as well as entrapment of antigens to expose them to immune cells. This class includes lymph nodes, the spleen and other masses of tissue such as the appendix, tonsils and mucosal tissue like Peyer's patches in the gut.

A major structure of interest are small masses of tissue, known as lymph nodes, that filter lymph.

A lymph node

An hereditary trait, or caused by extreme damage to the lymph vessels and particularly lymph nodes, lymphoedema is a swelling of tissue that affects 200 million humans, and cannot be treated (ref)

The spleen works similarly as a lymph node, but filters blood rather than lymph, and works to transfer lymphocytes into blood circulation.

Finally, some mucosal tissues can be considered as part of the lymphoid system, as they put lymphocytes in contact with antigens. A proper example are Peyer's patches, small masses of tissue situated in the lower intestine, that sample possible infectious agents and present them to the underlying cell-packed lymph.

Another function of the lymphatic circulation, and one of the first to be understood, is the transport of fat.

Indeed, intestinal villosities, the main exchange areas of the digestive system, are highly vascularised by blood capillaries, but also by lacteals, specific lymph capillaries that transport a type of lymph known as chyle, extremely rich in lipids.

Fun fact:
The origin of the word chyle is the greek word chylos, meaning juice.

As such, it can be said that the lymphatic system is an important part of the digestive system, which can itself be considered as another major transport system of animal organism.
