Context

In this analysis, weâre interested in biking patterns across Paris. The city hall installed sensors across Paris. Each of those sensors monitors the number of bikes passing in front of the sensor each day. Those daily tallys were recorded from the November 1st, 2019 to October 31st, 2020.

Weâll first describe the dataset using a few descriptive statistics before using it to study a few basic patterns:

  • are there more bikes on the streets in summer than in winter ?
  • are some directions more crowded than others ?

Import the data (1 pt)

The data are stored in three flat csv files located files located on moodle. Use read_csv() from the readr package to import them as counts, sensors and arrondissements. Each dataset consists of tabular data with several colums.

counts=read_csv("paris_bike_sensor_count_data_2019.csv")
sensors=read_csv("paris_bike_sensor_details.csv")
arrondissements=read_csv("arrondissement_details.csv")

arrondissements has 5 variables:

  • Arrondissement: district number
  • Arrondissement_name: district name
  • Surface: district surface (in square meters)
  • Perimeter: district perimeter (in meters)
  • Population: district legal population of 2020 (as per the INSEE definition)

counts has 3 variables:

  • ID: sensor unique ID
  • Date: date of tally
  • Bike_count: daily bike count

sensors has 7 variables

  • ID: sensor unique ID
  • Name: sensor name (usually a location and a direction)
  • Location_ID: sensor location unique ID
  • Location_ID: sensor location name
  • Setup_date: date of installation (and of first daily record)
  • GPS_coordinates: GPS coordinates
  • Arrondissement: Arrondissement where the sensor is located

ID may differ from Location_ID if two sensors are located in the same position but monitor flows running in opposing directions.

Creative space (4 pt)

Suggest an interesting visualisation and/or answer an interesting question using the data at hand

We are pretty curious as to why the proportion of westwards traffic is lower in the Totem 64 rue de Rivoli data.

Maybe itâs Parisâ topography?

Itâs not the data at hand per se, but the open data initiative of the city of Paris also delivers measurements of height relative to sea level at 1664134 points according to the General Levelling of France (NGF) network, in the plan-de-voirie-points-de-nivellement-etiquettes dataset. The plan was to construct a heatmap of paris altitude with ggplot2, and try to see whether or not the fact that more bikes went eastwards was due to it being a downhill ride. However, as the sheer size of the dataset kept crashing R, and this hypothesis seemed unlikely, we gave up. A solution for working with the data might have been to make a proper json request to the serverâs API, but this did not seem very relevant to the question.

A quick look at the description of sensors used given by the company in charge of the deployment in Paris tells us that the data relies on the âmagnetic signature of each bike wheelâ, and that the measures are generally taken by a belt that reaches a maximum of 1.5 meters of length. Knowing how there is two sensors at this location (O-E and E-O), and that the cycling lane is 4m wide, everything checks out. However, since the âdÃ©confinementâ on the 11/05/2020, the rue de Rivoli has been partially cut from car traffic. This means that, as can be seen on the image presented on this article, the lane previously reserved to automobiles has been allocated to the bike flow. As this was not the case when the sensor was put in place (2019-09-04 according to our sensors dataframe), the bikes passing on this newly opened lane could not have been measured by the sensors. The direction getting the lesser proportion seems to check out with that hypothesis, as this new lane allows westward traffic.

Letâs investigate this hypothesis with our R skills.

First, weâll create a column in our dataframe representing the confinment status.

riv<-data%>%filter(Location_name=="Totem 64 Rue de Rivoli")

    Date <= as.Date("2020-03-17")                                 ~ "pre",
