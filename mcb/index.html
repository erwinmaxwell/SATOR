                                                                          1. Intracellular compartments and protein sorting

                                                                          Course material overview

                                                                          Alberts : Chapter 12, pp. 641-648, 659-662, 670-682

                                                                          Cells are compartmentalized for organization and sorting of specific tasks. The compartments are called organelles. The compartments are separated by phospholipidic membranes, which contain a variety of proteins within them.

                                                                          Proteins are transported to and from compartments through 3 major mechanisms:

                                                                          • gated transport : active transport of proteins and RNA molecules between the cytosol and the nucleus through nuclear pore complexes in the nuclear envelope
                                                                          • transmembrane transport : transmembrane protein translocators directly transport specific proteins across a membrane from the cytosol into a space that is topologically distinct
                                                                          • vesicular transport : membrane-enclosed transport intermediates ferry proteins from one topologically equivalent compartment to another

                                                                          virtually all proteins destined for secretion or for the ER itself, the Golgi apparatus, the lysosomes, the endosomes, and the plasma membrane are first imported into the ER from the cytosol

                                                                          A few things are required for a protein to transfer :

                                                                          • a signal (tag) intrinsic to the protein
                                                                          • a receptor to recognise that signal and direct the protein to the correct membrane
                                                                          • a translocation machinery to pass the membrane
                                                                          • energy

                                                                          All proteins begin their synthesis in the cytosol. Proteins destined for the cytosol, mitochondria, nucleus or peroxisomes are synthesized by cytosolic ribosomes, then recognised and transferred by a variety of molecules (free-flowing importins or peroxins respectively for the nucleus and peroxisome, membrane bound complexes for the mitochondria etc). Proteins destined for membrane or secretory pathway can also be synthesized in the cytosol and directed (post-translational) or can be synthesized by ribosomes bound to the endoplasmic reticulum membrane during translation (co-translational) thanks to Signal Recognition Particles, as shown below.

                                                                          Sec-61 complex : protein translocator common on the endoplasmic reticulum

                                                                          Membrane proteins are integrated on the endoplasmic reticulum via various sets of signal sequences (see below)

                                                                          3. SDS-Page and Western blots | RAJ

                                                                          Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis can pretty quickly and cheaply separatesproteins by their sizes only.

                                                                          1. Heat up cell lysate with SDS (unfold protein into long polypeptide chain) and beta-mercaptoethanol (break disulphide bonds)
                                                                          2. Put heated sample on polyacrylamide gel (polymerisation can be controlled for different pore size and qualities)
                                                                          3. Put gel in conductive buffer and apply current of desired intensity (~100V)
                                                                          4. Simply stain gel for detection of bands, or transfer on nitrocellulose paper for immunoblotting (aka Western blot) if proper antigen (can also be analysed with mass spectroscopy)

                                                                          Western blotting consists of soaking nitrocellulose membrane in various solutions of antibodies to link antigen with Horseradish Peroxydase or Alkaline Phosphatase. Adding luminol and hydrogen peroxyde to a HRP blot will make it light up. Adding 5-bromo,4-chloro,3-indolyl phosphate (BCIP) and Nitroblue Tetrazolium (NBT) to a AP blot in alkaline conditions will make it color up.

                                                                          N.B. : Primary and Secondary antigens need to come from different animals

                                                                          5. Protein modification and quality control in the secretory pathway

                                                                          Alberts : Chapter 12 pp. 683-688 | Chapter 13 pp. 712, 715-716, 743-744

                                                                          We're interested in the various processing of proteins in the secretory pathway, and particularly in the Endoplasmic Reticulum.

                                                                          Cleavage of signal peptides

                                                                          As seen previously, proteins require a signal sequence for transport in the proper organelle. These peptides are generally cleaved off once the protein has reached its destination.

                                                                          Glycosylation

                                                                          = attaching a carbohydrate to a functional group of another molecule

                                                                          In the ER, glycosylation of proteins happens to facilitate folding, binding of chaperones and to limit access of proteases (increasing stability in the extracellular space). For some proteins, more extensive glycosylation is necessary, for protection against pathogens or even cell to cell adhesion (oligosaccharides).

                                                                          Glycosylation of proteins can occur on :

                                                                          • −OH group of serine or threonine = O-linked, happens in the Golgi
                                                                          • −NHX2 group of asparagine = N-linked, happens in the ER

                                                                          The oligosaccharide shown in the figure above is assembled inside the ER, on a lipid carrier molecule called dolichol, and then transferred on its target asparagine by an enzyme called oligosaccharide transferase.

                                                                          Formation of disulphide bonds

                                                                          Protein Disulfide Isomerase is an ER resident protein, involved in the formation of disulphide bonds within the ER lumen.

                                                                          Assembly of multi-subunit proteins

                                                                          Protein complexes made of multiple subunits are assembled in the ER.

                                                                          Proline isomeration

                                                                          Peptidyl-prolyl isomerase are very important enzymes, necessary for accelerating the naturally very slow isomerisation of proline residues (trans being the most common). Folding of certain proteins require certain isomers, and PPIase accelerates the process.

                                                                          6. Vesicle budding and fusion

                                                                          Alberts : Chapter 13 pp. 695-710, 727-730

                                                                          Budding

                                                                          Vesicle budding requires formation of a protein coat that selectively recruits specific cargo and provides curvature

                                                                          Different intracellular membranes present different phosphoinositide molecules, recognized by different coat proteins.

                                                                          The coat formation is initiated by small GTPases (i.e. SarI and ArfI), varying depending on the membrane, but that can recruit the same coat protein (i.e. clathrin) at different locations.

                                                                          Fusion

                                                                          Fusion of the vesicles requires monomeric Rab-GTPases to dock to specific membranes.

                                                                          Vesicle (v-) and transmembrane (t-) SNARE proteins allow the vesicle to tether to its target membrane.

                                                                          It can be added at any terminus of a protein to act as a reporter.

                                                                          9. Endocytosis

                                                                          Alberts : Chapter 13: Intracellular membrane traffic pp731 -742

                                                                          The vesicles used in the process of receptor-mediated endocytosis are called endosomes

                                                                          Low density lipoproteins (LDL) are one of the five groups of lipid transport particles in the body. It is transported into the cell through its binding to LDL receptors (LDLR).

                                                                          pH changes are an important trigger indicating localisation.

                                                                          Other example of endocytosis include Transferrin R for the transport of iron ions and EGFR for growth factors. EGFR are ubiquitinylated, which targets them to Multivesicular Bodies, where both the receptor and ligand are degraded.

                                                                          10. Studying the secretory and endocytic pathways

                                                                          Two techniques described previously are central to the study of the secretory and endocytic pathway :

                                                                          Immunofluorescence microscopy
                                                                          • To determine whether a mutation identified in a disease affects subcellular localisation
                                                                          • To determine whether a particular domain of a protein or signal sequence is required for recruitment to a particular compartment
                                                                          • To see how the localisation of a protein changes in response to a treatment or over time (e.g. depletion of a protein, growth factor stimulus, drug treatment)
                                                                          Western blotting
                                                                          • To determine the amount of a particular protein
                                                                          • To look at post-translational modification e.g. molecular weight change of glycosylated protein
                                                                          • Can look at cellular localisation combined with subcellular fractionation techniques

                                                                          Series 2 : The Cytoskeleton

                                                                          WEEK 15

                                                                          11. Intro to cytoskeleton

                                                                          The cytoskeleton is a dynamic network of filaments that provides the cell with shape, support, strength, organisation and movement.

                                                                          12. Microtubules

                                                                          Microtubules are polar polymers of α / β - tubulin dimers. They have fast growing + (finishing at β-tubulin) and slow growing - (finishing at α-tubulin) ends. They exhibit dynamic instability, powered by GTP hydrolysis.

                                                                          Microtubule Associated Proteins (e.g. stathmin) regulate stability and crosslinking.

                                                                          Motor proteins (dynein, kinesins) move cargoes along microtubules in both directions. They are responsible for moving and organising cell contents

                                                                          13. Actin

                                                                          Actin filaments are polymers of globular actin monomers. They are highly dynamic and undergo treadmilling, powered by ATP hydrolysis

                                                                          A range of actin binding proteins regulate stability and various forms of crosslinking

                                                                          Actin determines cell shape, formation of contractile structures and migration

                                                                          WEEK 17

                                                                          14. Intermediate filaments

                                                                          Intermediate filaments are rope-like polymers. They are flexible, strong and relatively stable. They provide resistance to mechanical strain.

                                                                          Many types of IF are expressed in different tissues.

                                                                          Nuclear lamins are a good example, present in most cells and allowing a variety of connections.

                                                                          Mutations in some IFs weaken the network and cause human diseases. Some filaments can be modulated by phosphorylation. For example, nuclear lamins are phosphorylated by Cdk1 during mitosis, allowing for nuclear wall breakdown.

                                                                          15. Analysing protein function | RAJ

                                                                          Protein function can be manipulated at:

                                                                          1. DNA level • Overexpression of wt gene • Overexpression of dominant active mutant • Overexpression of dominant negative (inactive) mutant
                                                                          2. mRNA level • Expression of mRNA • RNA interference
                                                                          3. Protein level • Chemical inhibitors

                                                                          Protein structure and function can be regulated by phosphorylation of serine, threonine and tyrosine residues. Changing these residues by negatively charged ones such as aspartic or glutamic acid can make them constitutively active. Alternatively, replacing them with uncharged and unphosphorylatable residues like alanine or phenylalanine will render them constitutively inactive.

                                                                          Over-expressing mutant proteins to interfere with a certain function is a widely used study method. Techniques used for such altering of protein functions can be gene cloning and transient transfection, or fancier like RNA interference (RNAi) :

                                                                          A specific double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) molecule is designed and introduced in the cell, where it is cleaved into small interfering RNA (siRNA) molecules by an enzyme known as Dicer. The two strands of those siRNA fragments are unwound and one of them binds to the RNA-induced silencing complex, which can then proceed to silence the targeted gene's expression by complementarily binding to a sequence of the corresponding mRNA and cleaving it by use of Argonaute 2, an enzyme of the RISC.

                                                                          Alternatively, chemical inhibitors can be used to target specific protein functions (e.g. Ro 3306 competing with ATP to inhibit Cdk1 activity).

                                                                          16. Determining cytoskeleton functions

                                                                          RNAi is much simpler and cheaper than breeding knock-out organisms.

                                                                          Chemical inhibitors of actin treadmilling such as Nocodazole and Cytochalasin D can be used to appreciate its properties and functions.

                                                                          Over-expressing functionless mutants such as headless motor proteins can prove to be a good specific method of study of intracellular transport.

                                                                          Mutating lamin's phosphorylatable residues with unphosphorylatable ones to create a constituvely inactive protein reveals its necessity for the depolymerisation of nuclear lamina.

                                                                          WEEK 18

                                                                          17. Intro to cell signalling

                                                                          Cell signalling is widespread and essential.

                                                                          Different kinds of signal receptors exist.

                                                                          Conformational changes activate signal transduction.

                                                                          18+19. Phosphorylation in signalling

                                                                          Only serine, threonine and tyrosine residues can get phosphorylated.

                                                                          A variety of specific kinases exist.

                                                                          Receptor tyrosine kinases' activation depends on dimerization.

                                                                          Phosphorylation events create docking sites for further transduction. RTKs display phosphotyrosines, which have high affinity for receptor domains such as SH2 and PTB (which recognise the residue and a particular surrounding amino acid sequence), turning the activated receptor into a signalling hub.

                                                                          Mutating phosphorylatable residues into charged (called a phosphomimetic substitution) or unphosphorylatable (called non-phosphorylatable substitution) amino acids is a good technique for study of signalling pathways.

                                                                          20. Methods for studying protein-protein interactions | RAJ

                                                                          Protein-protein interactions are critical in virtually all cellular processes.

                                                                          Methods to study protein-protein interactions include: affinity chromatography (pull-down assay) and co-immunoprecipitation.

                                                                          These techniques may be used for identifying unknown protein binding partners (using Mass Spectrometry) or characterising known protein-protein interactions (using Western botting).

                                                                          21. Regulation of signalling output

                                                                          Monomeric GTPases are common molecular switches in cell signalling.

                                                                          MAPK cascades are a highly conserved signalling component consisting of three-tiered protein kinases

                                                                          Mutations of Ras and Raf proteins is a common cause of cancer.

                                                                          Different ERK activities result in different cellular response.

                                                                          Positive and negative feedback circuits shape signalling outputs.

                                                                           

                                                                          Series 4 : The Cell Division Cycle

                                                                          WEEK 20

                                                                          22. Stages of the eukaryotic cell cycle

                                                                          The eukaryotic cell cycle is required for unicellular propagation and development of multicellular organisms.

                                                                          The cell cycle is divided into specific cell cycle stages with abrupt transitions between one stage and the next

                                                                          Chromosome segregation occurs on a microtubule-based bipolar mitotic spindle.

                                                                          Cell fusion experiments revealed internal regulators of the cell cycle termed MPF and SPF. Both are Cyclin-dependent kinase (Cdk) - cyclin complexes.

                                                                          Mitotic cells contain the M-phase promoting factor (MPF), an activator that will push a cell in any phase of the cell cycle directly into mitosis. S-phase cells contain the S-phase promoting factor (SPF), an activator that push G1 phase cells into S-phase. G2 phase cells are insensitive to the SPF. The DNA does not re-replicate before mitosis. This is a critical control. G1 and G2 phase cells do not contain either the MPF or SPF (at least in an active state).

                                                                          23. Principles of cell cycle control

                                                                          MPF and SPF are examples of CDK/cyclin complexes that drive transitions between different cell cycle stages. CDK are small (~34kDa) serine/threonine kinases that need cyclin to get into an active conformation.

                                                                          The activity of CDKs is controlled through the cyclical synthesis and degradation of the respective cyclin subunit.

                                                                          Different CDK/cyclin complexes control passage through different transitions of the cell cycle.

                                                                          24. Flow cytometry and cell cycle analysis | RAJ

                                                                          1. Biophysical technique : the flow cytometer produces a single line of cells that are interrogated by a laser. The refracted light or fluorescent light is detected by a specific detector(s).

                                                                          1. Applications : cell number, cell identification, protein/DNA levels & protein/DNA post-translational modifications in single cells.

                                                                          1. Cell cycle analysis : flow cytometry is widely used to analyse cell cycle progression by analysing the DNA content of single cells using the DNA-binding dye Propidium Iodide.

                                                                          25+26. Regulation of CDKs

                                                                          Cyclins lead to allosteric activation of CDKs, moving the T-loop out of the catalytic cleft, rotating the PSTAIRE helix and allowing Glu-51 and Lys-33 to form a salt bridge that enables presentation of the g-P of ATP for nucleophilic attack.

                                                                          CDKs are regulated by both activating and inhibitory phosphorylation events

                                                                          CDK alone is not phosphorylated. Upon binding of cyclin, structural changes in the CDK molecule expose three sites for phosphorylation :

                                                                          • Thr-161 (in T-loop) is phosphorylated by CAK (CDK activating kinase) to stabilise the T-loop in the open (active) conformation.
                                                                          • Thr-14 and Tyr-15 are phosphorylated by Wee1 (these are inhibitory) repelling ATP from the active site. Upon activation of Cdc25, this phosphatase dephosphorylates Thr-14 and Tyr-15 to activate CDK.

                                                                          Therefore the timing of mitotic entry depends upon what triggers the activation of Cdc25 – this is the “rate limiting step”. => The Cdc25 phosphatase is the rate limiting “activator” of CDKs.

                                                                          G1 and G1/S CDKs are also negatively regulated by CDK Inhibitor proteins (CKI). They come in two families :

                                                                          • the p16 (INK4) type CKIs which inhibit CDK4 and CDK6
                                                                          • the p21 and p27 type CKIs which inhibit CDK2.

                                                                          Cyclin degradation is mediated by the 26S proteasome following ubiquitylation by an E3 ubiquitin ligase.

                                                                          Ubiquitination for Cyclin Proteolysis

                                                                          Cyclin Proteolysis' regulation of CDK activity

                                                                           

                                                                          Active CDKs phosphorylate a wide range of substrates at a particular target consensus sequence.

                                                                          Phosphorylation and dephosphorylation of CDK substrates triggers the events associated with cell cycle transitions. At the G2/M transition, phosphorylation of substrates by CDK1 (cdc2) leads to the reorganization of the cell to form a mitotic spindle. At the M/G1 transition, dephosphorylation of those same substrates leads to the return of the cell to an interphase state. Other CDK substrates include nuclear lamins (disassembly of the nuclear envelope), motor proteins (spindle organization), histones (chromatin condensation) and nucleolin (cessation of rRNA synthesis).

                                                                          27. Cell cycle checkpoints

                                                                          Cell cycle checkpoints ensure that passage through the cell cycle is carried out in an orderly and timely fashion.

                                                                          Checkpoint pathways consist of sensors, signalling pathways and effectors that act to stop cell cycle progression.

                                                                          Loss of checkpoints is a key step in the development of cancer.
